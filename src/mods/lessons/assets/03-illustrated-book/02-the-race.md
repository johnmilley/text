At sunrise the fox dropped a leaf to start them, and the hare was gone
before it touched the ground — a blur of dust, already halfway to the first
bend while the tortoise was still finding his stride.

By the time the tortoise reached the bend himself, the hare had stopped.
"I'm so far ahead," the hare reasoned, "that I could sleep through the
whole morning and still win by noon." He found a warm, flat stone under a
tree and did exactly that.

The tortoise didn't stop. He didn't speed up either — there wasn't a faster
gear to find — he simply kept the same pace he'd started with, past the
bend, past the sleeping hare, past the long shadow of the oak tree coming
into view ahead.

The hare woke to the sound of the other animals cheering — not for him.
